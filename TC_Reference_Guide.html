Section 1

Who Is Who in a Real Estate Transaction

Every transaction involves the same core group of people. Knowing who each person is and what their job is will help you understand why a document belongs to one person and not another — and who to contact when something needs to happen.

1 The Seller

The seller is the person (or people) who currently owns the property and wants to sell it. They sign the listing agreement, the disclosures, and the final contract. On the checklist, any time you see "seller signs" or "seller confirms" — that's this person.

On a DBRE seller-side transaction, the seller is our client. We represent them.

2 The Buyer

The buyer is the person who wants to purchase the property. They sign the offer, the buyer agency agreement, and the disclosures. On a DBRE buyer-side transaction, the buyer is our client. We represent them.

3 The Listing Agent / Listing Firm

The listing agent (also called the seller's agent) is the real estate advisor who represents the seller. The listing firm is the company that agent works for. On DBRE seller-side transactions, DBRE is the listing firm and the advisor on the checklist is the listing agent.

Note: You will see "listing agent" and "seller's agent" used interchangeably throughout real estate documents. They mean the same thing.
4 The Buyer's Agent / Selling Firm

The buyer's agent (also called the selling agent or cooperating agent) is the real estate advisor who represents the buyer. The firm they work for is called the selling firm or cooperating firm.

Important — These terms are confusing: "Selling agent" does NOT mean the agent selling the home. It means the agent who brought the buyer to the sale. "Listing agent" is the one who listed the home for sale. "Selling agent" is the one who sold it to their buyer client. These terms are used interchangeably in real estate documents — when you see "selling firm" or "cooperating firm," it always means the buyer's agent's firm.

On DBRE buyer-side transactions, DBRE is the selling/cooperating firm and the advisor is the buyer's agent.

5 The Transaction Coordinator (TC)

That's you. Your job is to manage the paperwork and the timeline from contract to close. You make sure every document is collected, signed, uploaded to the right gallery, and that deadlines are tracked. You do not advise clients — that's the advisor's job. You manage the file.

6 The Closing Attorney / Settlement Agent

In North Carolina, all real estate closings must be handled by a licensed attorney. The closing attorney (also called the settlement agent) handles the legal transfer of the property, collects and distributes all funds, prepares the closing documents, and records the deed. They are a neutral party — they represent neither the buyer nor the seller. They represent the transaction itself.

The earnest money is held by the closing attorney. The commission is paid out through the closing attorney at the closing table.

7 The Lender

If the buyer is financing the purchase (taking out a loan), the lender is the bank or mortgage company providing that loan. The lender orders the appraisal, approves the buyer's financing, and issues the Clear to Close before closing day. On cash transactions, there is no lender.

Section 2

How Commission Works

Commission is one of the most confusing parts of real estate for anyone new to the industry. This section explains how it works step by step, using real numbers, and then explains exactly which documents cover which part of the commission arrangement.

1 The Basic Idea

Commission is the fee paid to the real estate firms involved in the transaction. It is almost always paid by the seller out of the proceeds from the sale. It is calculated as a percentage of the sale price.

There are two firms involved in most transactions: the listing firm (represents the seller) and the selling/cooperating firm (represents the buyer). Each firm earns a portion of the total commission.

2 How the Split Works — Worked Example
Example — $300,000 Sale Price

Step 1 — DBRE charges the seller 6% total commission
6% of $300,000 = $18,000 total commission paid by seller at closing

Step 2 — DBRE (as listing firm) offers to share part of that with the buyer's agent's firm
DBRE offers 2.5% to the cooperating firm (buyer's agent's firm)
2.5% of $300,000 = $7,500 paid to the buyer's agent's firm

Step 3 — DBRE keeps the remainder
6% − 2.5% = 3.5% kept by DBRE (listing firm)
3.5% of $300,000 = $10,500 kept by DBRE

This is what Form 220 documents. Form 220 (Cooperating Compensation Agreement) is the agreement between the listing firm (DBRE) and the selling/cooperating firm (buyer's agent's firm) that documents what the listing firm has agreed to pay the cooperating firm. It has nothing to do with what the buyer pays.
3 When the Buyer Owes a Portion — The Gap

Sometimes the buyer's agent charges their client more than what the listing firm has agreed to pay. When that happens, the buyer is responsible for paying the difference — the "gap." This is covered in the Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement.

Example — Buyer Responsible for the Gap

The buyer's agent charges their client 3% (per their Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement)

The listing firm offers to pay only 2.5% (per Form 220)

The gap = 3% − 2.5% = 0.5%

On a $300,000 sale: 0.5% of $300,000 = $1,500 owed by the buyer

This $1,500 is paid by the buyer at closing — it is NOT the total commission. It is only the portion not covered by the listing firm's offer.

This is the most common confusion — remember this: When you see a buyer responsibility for a commission amount on a document, that is NEVER the total commission. It is only the gap between what the buyer's agent charges and what the listing firm agreed to pay. The total commission (paid by the seller) is a completely separate, much larger number.

5 How Commission Is Paid

Commission is not paid directly by anyone to anyone during the transaction. It is all handled at the closing table by the closing attorney. Here is the flow:

Commission Flow at Closing

1. The buyer brings their funds (down payment + closing costs + any gap amount) to the closing attorney

2. The lender wires the loan proceeds to the closing attorney (if financed)

3. On a cash transaction, the buyer wires their full purchase funds directly to the closing attorney

4. The closing attorney deducts all costs including commission from the seller's proceeds

5. The closing attorney writes checks (or wires) to: the listing firm (DBRE), the cooperating firm, the seller (their net proceeds)

6. The deed is recorded and the property officially transfers to the buyer

DBRE-Specific: After closing, Derrick coordinates the commission disbursement and posts the commission statement to the advisor's boardroom. This is Derrick's responsibility — it is not part of the TC's post-closing tasks.
Section 3

Due Diligence & Earnest Money

Due diligence and earnest money are two separate payments the buyer makes when an offer is accepted. They are often confused because they happen at the same time — but they are very different in terms of what they mean, where they go, and what happens to them.

1 Due Diligence Fee — The Buyer's "Right to Investigate"

When a buyer's offer is accepted, the buyer has a window of time called the due diligence period to investigate the property — inspections, appraisals, surveys, soil tests (for land), etc. During this window, the buyer has the right to walk away from the deal for any reason at all.

The due diligence fee is what the buyer pays the seller for that right. Think of it as renting the right to investigate the property before committing fully.

Key facts about the DD fee:
• Paid directly to the seller — not to the attorney
• Non-refundable — if the buyer walks away during DD, the seller keeps it
• Negotiated as part of the offer — amount varies by transaction
• On residential: paid at contract execution, typically within 24–48 hours
• On land: same concept, but the DD period is usually longer because studies (soil, survey, environmental) take more time
2 Earnest Money — The Buyer's "Good Faith Deposit"

Earnest money is a deposit the buyer pays to show they are serious about completing the purchase. The amount varies — sometimes earnest money is larger than the DD fee, and sometimes the DD fee is larger. It depends on what is negotiated in the offer. Unlike the DD fee, earnest money is held by the closing attorney in a trust account — it is not given to the seller yet.

Key facts about earnest money:
• Paid to the closing attorney — held in trust (escrow)
• Applied toward the buyer's costs at closing if the deal closes
• Refundable if the buyer terminates during the due diligence period
• At risk if the buyer backs out after the DD period expires without a valid reason
• Amount is negotiated — typically 1–2% of purchase price on residential; varies on land

4 The Due Diligence Period — What Happens During It

The DD period runs from the date the contract is executed (signed by all parties) to the DD deadline date listed on the contract. During this window:

Residential Transactions

• Buyer schedules home inspection — inspector checks structure, systems, roof, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, etc.

• Buyer's lender orders appraisal (if financed)

• Advisor handles any repair negotiations with the seller — TC uploads the executed repair agreement once signed

• Buyer decides: proceed to close, negotiate repairs/price, or terminate

Land Transactions

• Buyer arranges soil/perk test (to determine if land can support a septic system)

• Buyer orders boundary or topographic survey

• Buyer confirms zoning, flood zone, wetlands, DOT access, utilities

• There are NO repair requests on land — buyer investigates and either proceeds or terminates

• DD period is typically longer than residential because studies take more time

DBRE-Specific — Your TC role at DD expiration: Three days before the DD deadline, you send an alert to the advisor. On the DD deadline itself, you confirm with the advisor whether the buyer is proceeding or terminating. If proceeding, the transaction continues. If terminating, the contract ends and you document accordingly.
Section 4

Key Documents — What Each One Is

Every document in the checklist exists for a specific reason. This section explains what each one does in plain language so you understand why it belongs in a particular gallery and why the closing attorney or advisor might ask for it.

1 Agency & Representation Documents
Both Sides
Working with Real Estate Agents Brochure (WWREA)
A state-required brochure that explains the different types of agency relationships in North Carolina — buyer's agent, seller's agent, dual agent, etc. Must be reviewed with every client at first substantial contact. The client signs it to confirm they received and reviewed it. Required on both buyer and seller sides.
Seller Side
Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement
The contract between the seller and DBRE (as the listing firm) that gives DBRE the exclusive right to market and sell the property. It states the listing price, listing period, and the total commission the seller agrees to pay DBRE. This is what makes DBRE the official listing firm for the property.
Buyer Side
Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement
The contract between the buyer and DBRE (as the buyer's firm) that establishes DBRE's representation of the buyer. It states what fee DBRE charges for buyer representation services and that the buyer will cover any portion of that fee not paid by the listing firm. This is NOT the same as Form 220.
2 Commission Documents
Both Sides
Cooperating Compensation Form 220
The agreement between the listing firm and the buyer's agent's firm (cooperating/selling firm) documenting how much the listing firm will pay the cooperating firm from the commission. All four parties sign this: the seller, the listing advisor/DBRE, the buyer, and the buyer's agent/firm. The seller and buyer sign it even though it documents an arrangement between the two firms — it is a required acknowledgement that all parties are aware of the compensation agreement. It appears in both seller and buyer checklists. Important: this is NOT the total commission — it is only the listing firm's offer to the cooperating firm.
Both Sides
Professional Services Disclosure and Election (Form 760)
A disclosure form that documents what professional services are being performed in the transaction and by whom. Required in North Carolina for agency relationships. DBRE standard: required on all transactions — not advisor discretion. If this form is missing from a file, flag it to the advisor immediately.
3 Disclosure Documents
Seller Side
Residential Property Disclosure Statement (RPOADS)
A form the seller completes disclosing known issues with the property — roof condition, water damage, foundation issues, HVAC, appliances, etc. The seller fills this out honestly. The buyer reviews and signs it to confirm they received it. Residential transactions only.
Seller Side
Vacant Land Disclosure Statement
The land equivalent of the RPOADS. The seller discloses known information about the land — zoning, utilities, environmental issues, easements, etc. Land transactions only.
Both Sides
Mineral, Oil, and Gas Rights Disclosure (MOG)
A NC-required disclosure that informs the buyer whether the seller owns the mineral, oil, and gas rights to the property — or whether those rights have been separated from the land. Required on both residential and land transactions, for both buyer and seller sides.
Seller Side
Lead-Based Paint Addendum
Required only on homes built before 1978. Federal law requires sellers to disclose known lead-based paint hazards. The buyer has the right to test for lead-based paint during the DD period.
4 Contract Documents
Buyer Side
Offer to Purchase and Contract — Residential (Form 2-T)
The main purchase contract for residential property. Once signed by all parties, this is the binding agreement. It contains the purchase price, DD fee, earnest money, DD deadline, closing date, and all terms. This is what makes the transaction "under contract."
Buyer Side
Vacant Lot/Land Offer to Purchase and Contract (Form 12-T)
The land equivalent of Form 2-T. Same purpose — the binding purchase contract — but specific to vacant lot and land transactions. Contains legal description, acreage, and parcel number in addition to the standard contract terms.
Buyer Side
Due Diligence Repair Request (Form 310-T)
Residential only. After the home inspection, the buyer may request that the seller make specific repairs before closing. This form documents those requests. The seller can agree, negotiate, or decline. Whatever is agreed upon becomes a binding addendum to the contract. Does NOT exist on land transactions — there are no repair requests on land.
5 Closing Documents
Both Sides
HUD Settlement Statement / Closing Disclosure (CD)
The final financial summary of the entire transaction prepared by the closing attorney. Shows every dollar coming in and going out — purchase price, loan amount, closing costs, commission, prorations, and the seller's net proceeds. On financed transactions this is called the Closing Disclosure (CD). On cash transactions and land it may be an ALTA Settlement Statement. This goes in Heritage Gallery (04) after closing.
Buyer Side
Buyer Engagement Letter
A letter from the closing attorney to the buyer confirming the attorney has been engaged to handle the closing and outlining the attorney's role. The buyer signs this and it goes to Legacy Gallery (03).

2 Commission — The 60-Second Summary
Remember this every time you see commission numbers:

Form 220 = agreement between listing firm and buyer's agent's firm only. Has nothing to do with what the buyer owes directly.

Buyer Agency Agreement = what the buyer's firm charges the buyer. If the listing firm doesn't cover the full amount, the buyer pays the gap.

If you see a buyer responsible for 0.5% — that is NOT the total commission. It is only the gap. Total commission (paid by seller) is a separate, much larger number.

All commission is distributed by the closing attorney at the closing table. No firm receives commission directly from the buyer or seller during the transaction.
3 DD Fee vs. Earnest Money — The 30-Second Summary
DD Fee — paid to the seller, non-refundable, buys the buyer the right to investigate and walk away

Earnest Money — paid to the closing attorney, held in trust, refundable if buyer terminates during DD period, applied to buyer's costs at closing

Both require a signed Acknowledgement of Receipt — that's your job to obtain and upload to Legacy Gallery (03)
