02  ·  Step by Step

The Land Buying Process

Purchasing land involves a different process than buying a home — and a different mindset. Here is what to expect from first conversation to closing day.

1

Buyer Consultation

We begin here. This conversation is where we learn your goals, intended use, timeline, and budget. We walk you through the land buying process, identify what you need to evaluate in any parcel you consider, and make sure we are the right fit for each other.

2

Define Your Criteria

Land searches require more specificity than residential searches. Your advisor helps you articulate what matters — acreage range, zoning requirements, location, utility needs, intended use, and budget — so we are focused from the start and not wasting time on parcels that cannot serve your goals.

3

Financing Preparation

Land financing works differently than residential mortgages. Before searching in earnest, your advisor will discuss your financing approach — cash, land loan, construction loan, or owner financing — so your offer is credible and your expectations are aligned with what lenders will require.

4

Property Search & Evaluation

Your advisor searches the MLS and available off-market sources for parcels that meet your criteria. For each property of interest, we review available documentation — surveys, plats, zoning records, perc test results — and help you ask the right questions before you invest time or money in deeper investigation.

5

Making an Offer

When you find the right parcel, your advisor prepares a competitive offer. Land offers typically include a due diligence period long enough for you to complete soil testing, survey review, and financing arrangements. We negotiate terms that protect your ability to investigate thoroughly before committing.

6

Due Diligence

This is the most critical phase of a land purchase. Your advisor coordinates and guides your investigation — soil and perc testing, survey review, zoning verification, utility assessment, flood zone and wetland review, and any other parcel-specific concerns. You will not proceed past this phase without a clear picture of what you are buying.

7

Closing Day

Once due diligence is complete and financing is arranged, your closing attorney coordinates the final documents. You sign, the deed is recorded, and the land is yours. Your advisor is with you through the finish line.

03  ·  Know Before You Offer

What to Evaluate in Any Land Purchase

Every land parcel is different. Unlike a home, where a standard inspection covers most critical systems, land requires you to investigate factors that are less visible — and sometimes less obvious. Here is what your advisor will help you evaluate.

FactorWhat You Need to Know
Soil & Perc TestingIf public sewer is not available, you will need a septic system — and the land must pass a percolation (perc) test to confirm it can support one. This test is typically completed during due diligence. A failed perc test does not automatically disqualify a parcel, but it changes your options and your costs significantly. Your advisor will discuss the test process and what results mean for your intended use.
Zoning & Permitted UsesZoning determines what you are legally allowed to build and how. Before you fall in love with a parcel, confirm that your intended use — residential home, accessory dwelling, agricultural use, commercial structure — is permitted under current zoning. Your advisor will help you verify this and identify whether a rezoning or variance would be needed.
Survey & BoundariesA current survey tells you exactly what you are buying — where the boundaries are, what is inside them, and whether there are encroachments. If no current survey exists, ordering one during due diligence is strongly recommended. Do not close on land without understanding your boundaries.
Utilities & InfrastructureIs public water or sewer available at the road? Is electrical service accessible? What about natural gas? If utilities are not available, what will it cost to bring them in — or to build around their absence with well, septic, and alternative energy? These costs can be substantial and should be factored into your offer and your overall budget.
Road Access & EasementsDoes the parcel have direct frontage on a public road, or does access require crossing another property via an easement? Are there existing easements — utility lines, drainage corridors, shared driveways — that affect what you can build and where? Your advisor will review deed records and survey documents to identify any access or easement issues.
Flood Zone & WetlandsFEMA flood zone designation affects your buildable area, your insurance costs, and what lenders will require. Jurisdictional wetlands — regulated by the Army Corps of Engineers — may limit where you can build or require permits. Both should be researched before you commit. Your advisor will help you identify and evaluate these factors for any parcel you are considering.
Deed Restrictions & CovenantsSome parcels carry deed restrictions or HOA covenants that limit what can be built, what the land can be used for, or how it must be maintained. These restrictions run with the land and are binding on future owners. Review them carefully before closing.
Topography & ClearingIs the land flat or does it have significant grade changes that would require grading and fill? Is it wooded, cleared, or mixed? Clearing and grading costs can add meaningfully to your development budget — particularly on larger or more heavily wooded parcels.
Timber & Mineral RightsIn some transactions, timber rights or mineral rights have been severed from the surface estate — meaning a prior owner retained rights you may not be purchasing. Your advisor and closing attorney will review the deed history to ensure you understand what you are and are not acquiring.
DOT & Right-of-Way ExposurePlanned road projects, highway widening, or state DOT right-of-way acquisition can reduce a parcel's usable area — sometimes substantially — and affect its long-term value. Your advisor will research known DOT activity affecting any parcel you are seriously considering and factor it into your evaluation.
Future Development in ProximityWhat gets built near your land matters. Planned subdivisions, commercial development, industrial facilities, or new infrastructure can affect privacy, noise, traffic, and the character of your intended use. Your advisor will review county planning records for nearby development activity so you can make your decision with full context.
Do Not Skip Due Diligence on Land

Land due diligence is not optional — it is where the real work happens. Unlike a home inspection, which can often be completed in a few hours, land due diligence can involve multiple vendors, government offices, and potentially weeks of review. Your advisor will help you build a realistic due diligence plan before you make an offer, so you have the time you need to investigate properly.

04  ·  Under Contract

Due Diligence & Your Right to Terminate

In North Carolina, South Carolina, and Georgia, land contracts include a due diligence period — your window to investigate the property and terminate for any reason before the deadline. This protection is one of the most important elements of your offer.

ItemDetails
Due Diligence PeriodA negotiated window — often 30 to 60 days for land — during which you have the right to investigate the property and terminate the contract for any reason. This period must be long enough for you to complete soil testing, survey review, utility assessment, and financing arrangements.
Due Diligence FeeA negotiated fee paid directly to the seller at contract execution — non-refundable, but credited at closing if you proceed. This is your cost for taking the property off the market while you investigate.
Earnest MoneyA good-faith deposit held in escrow — typically refundable if you terminate within the due diligence period. If you proceed past due diligence and later terminate without contractual basis, earnest money may be at risk.
Key DeadlineWritten notice of termination must be delivered before the due diligence period expires. Do not assume verbal communication is sufficient — your advisor will ensure termination is handled in writing and on time if you choose not to proceed.
Contingency-Based OffersIn some land transactions, buyers request contingencies tied to specific outcomes — a passing perc test, rezoning approval, or subdivision plat approval. Whether a seller accepts these contingencies is negotiable. Your advisor will discuss when to pursue them and when to structure your offer differently.

What to Complete During Due Diligence

Your advisor will help you build a due diligence checklist specific to the parcel. Depending on the property, this may include soil and perc testing, a new or updated survey, zoning verification with the local planning office, utility availability confirmation, flood zone and wetland review, deed restriction and covenant review, DOT and right-of-way research, county planning records review for nearby development, and title examination by your closing attorney. Not every item applies to every parcel — but your advisor will help you determine what is essential for yours.

05  ·  Financing Your Land Purchase

How Land Financing Works

Land does not finance the same way a home does. Standard residential mortgages typically do not apply to vacant land. Understanding your options before you search is essential — it shapes what you can offer and how quickly you can move.

Financing TypeWhat to Know
Cash PurchaseThe simplest and most competitive option for land. Cash buyers face no appraisal requirements, no lender timelines, and no financing contingencies — which makes your offer stronger and your closing timeline more flexible. If this is an option for you, your advisor will help you use it strategically.
Land LoanSome lenders offer loans specifically for vacant land — typically with higher down payment requirements (20–50%) and higher interest rates than residential mortgages. Lender requirements vary significantly. If you are pursuing a land loan, connect with a lender who specializes in this type of financing before you begin searching.
Construction LoanIf you plan to build on the land, a construction-to-permanent loan may allow you to finance both the land purchase and the construction cost in a single loan product. These loans are more complex and require a defined build plan. Your advisor can connect you with lenders who offer this product.
Owner FinancingIn some land transactions — particularly rural acreage or parcels that are difficult to finance conventionally — the seller may offer to carry financing directly. Terms vary widely. If owner financing is part of an offer, your advisor will help you evaluate the terms and ensure the arrangement is properly documented.
USDA & Farm Credit LoansFor larger rural parcels or agricultural land, USDA loan programs and Farm Credit lenders may offer financing options not available through conventional lenders. Your advisor will help you identify whether your intended parcel and use case may qualify.
Know Your Financing Before You Offer

Unlike a home purchase where pre-approval is a straightforward first step, land financing requires more advance planning. Lender requirements vary, timelines are longer, and some parcels are simply not financeable through conventional channels. Your advisor will ask about your financing approach early — not to be intrusive, but because it directly shapes your offer strategy and your due diligence timeline.

07  ·  Buyer Discovery

Getting to Know You

Land purchases are highly individual — intended use shapes everything about the search and the due diligence process. Your advisor will ask questions like these during our consultation. There are no wrong answers.

Your Goals & Intended Use

  • What do you intend to do with the land — build a primary residence, a vacation or second home, an investment property, an agricultural operation, or something else?
  • Do you have a specific build plan in mind, or are you still evaluating options and looking for the right parcel first?
  • Is this a near-term purchase with immediate development plans, or are you acquiring land as a longer-term hold?
  • Have you purchased land before, or is this your first time navigating a land transaction?

Property Criteria

  • What is your target acreage range — are you looking for a single residential lot, a multi-acre parcel, or something larger?
  • Are there specific areas, corridors, or communities you are drawn to — and anywhere you have already ruled out?
  • Do you have requirements around utilities — public water and sewer, or are you comfortable with well and septic?
  • Are there any non-negotiables — road frontage, minimum acreage, distance to amenities, flood zone status — that would take a parcel off the table immediately?
  • How important is the ability to subdivide the parcel in the future, either for sale or for additional structures?

Financing

  • How are you planning to finance the purchase — cash, a land loan, a construction loan, or another approach?
  • If you are financing, have you spoken with a lender who specializes in land or construction loans, and do you have a sense of your budget ceiling?
  • If you plan to build, do you have a builder relationship in place, or will identifying a builder be part of the process?

The Process

  • How do you prefer to communicate — calls, texts, email? How quickly do you typically respond, and how much detail do you like in updates?
  • Are there other people involved in this decision — a partner, family member, builder, or financial advisor — who should be part of our conversations?
  • Is there anything about the land buying process that feels unclear or that you would like us to walk through in detail today?
